    Analyzing the Harry Styles fandom and Social Identity Theory

    Social identity theory is the idea that we each fall into a multitude of groups that form our unique identity and that these groups change how we view ourselves and others. Through categorization, identification, and comparison we are able to define who we are and which places we feel we belong (Trepte & Loy, 2017, p. 1). This applies heavily to entertainment media when we view how media figures influence how we behave, interact with others, and think of others. The core of social identity theory relies on there being an in-group and an out-group, which can become problematic in some areas of fandoms (Stets & Burke, 2000, p. 225). Fandom culture and parasocial relationship theory are based on the identification with certain figures, and how this contributes to our self-concept. Both fandoms and parasocial relationships can increase a sense of belonging as well as promote communities amongst their fans. For our evaluation of social identity theory with its ties to fandoms and parasocial relationships, we wanted to dive into the fandom of Harry Styles and how it shapes who they are, especially after the release of his latest album.

    One interesting way we see Harry Styles fans incorporate being a HS fan into their identity is the way they present themselves online. It's easy to find content for any media figure online, especially for Harry. Scrolling through an Instagram feed it's so easy to find reels of edits of Harry himself, compilations of people's experience at concerts, and even posts where you pick what kind of girl you are based on his songs aesthetics. When fans feel like they are part of this community and their identity is properly represented they "reap the benefits of inclusion and seek to engage more fully in fandom-related pursuits" (Groene & Hettinger, 2016, p. 335).

    Members of fandoms tend to share similar interests and traits that help them identify within said group. Particularly within the Harry Styles fandom, fans have a shared love of his music but tend to connect with him on a deeper psychological level, however this can sometimes have "notable cognitive, emotional, and behavioral ramifications" (Cohen & Bobitt, 2025, p. 90). Our identity is fluid and our likes, interests, and beliefs shift to better reflect and affirm who we are, which for fans who have a parasocial relationship with Styles, these characteristics might be influenced by him. This varies from simply relating to and finding comfort in his lyrics to enjoying watching what he does in his daily life and the beliefs he stands for. Since many people have grown up listening to him from One Direction, many fans feel like they have a good read on his character and who he is, and it is interesting to see how this impacts how fans react to certain things he does or what comes out about his personal life. For years, people have speculated who he is dating, what his sexuality is, and other details about his personal life, and recently there have been certain rumors going around that he is engaged, which regardless if he is or isn't, fans have been sharing their discontent with it.

    Many fans have found real community within the fandom. Particularly, attending a HS concert has been described as one big party where everyone feels free to dance and be themselves freely. This can be attributed to Styles' encouragement to just have fun and share love, but the fans have followed through by creating spaces in their real life and on social media where they feel supported and united by this love for Harry Styles. In Abigail Garrett's masters thesis she writes, "fans find comfort in one another when Harry is not available to provide the emotional support that these parasocial relationships rely on," which is such an interesting interpretation of relationships and community building like we see with this particular fandom (Garrett, 2022, p. 6).
